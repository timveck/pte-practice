Foundation Drills

Easy · ~20–25 min

Start with short, clear sentences. The first set focuses intensively on "that". The second set is authentic PTE-style with natural frequency. Finish with image descriptions.

🔹 Intensive "That" Practice (High Repetition)

🎯 Focus: Say "that" clearly, not "dat". Read each sentence smoothly in one breath.

📄 Authentic PTE-Style Passages (Natural "That" Frequency)

🎯 Realistic reading: These dense, academic passages are representative of PTE Reading and Listening transcripts.
Passage A · Behavioural Economics
The field of behavioural economics has challenged traditional assumptions about human decision-making. Classical economic models are built on the premise that individuals act rationally, weighing costs and benefits to maximise their utility. However, a growing body of research demonstrates that human behaviour is often influenced by cognitive biases and emotional factors. These findings have significant implications for public policy, particularly in areas such as retirement savings and healthcare choices. By understanding these biases, policymakers can design interventions that nudge citizens towards more beneficial outcomes.
Passage B · Urban Ecology
Urban ecology is an emerging discipline that examines the relationship between living organisms and their built environments. Unlike traditional ecology, which focuses on natural ecosystems, urban ecology considers the unique conditions created by human activity. These include altered temperature patterns, pollution levels, and habitat fragmentation. Recent studies have shown that cities can support remarkable biodiversity, provided that appropriate green infrastructure is maintained. This has led to a growing interest in designing cities that integrate natural systems into urban planning.
Passage C · Language Acquisition
The process of language acquisition in children has been the subject of extensive research for decades. While early theories emphasised the role of imitation and reinforcement, more recent perspectives highlight the innate capacity of the human brain to learn language. Researchers have identified specific neural pathways that are activated during language learning. The critical period hypothesis suggests that there is an optimal window for acquiring language, after which it becomes considerably more difficult. This has important implications for education and early childhood development.
Passage D · Global Supply Chains
Global supply chains have become increasingly complex and interconnected. A single product may involve components from dozens of countries, each with its own regulatory and logistical challenges. The recent pandemic exposed vulnerabilities in these networks, leading to widespread shortages and delays. In response, many corporations are now re-evaluating their reliance on distant suppliers. Some are adopting regional supply chain models, which offer greater resilience and shorter delivery times. The transition, however, is not without its costs.
Passage E · Artificial Intelligence Ethics
The ethical implications of artificial intelligence are a topic of intense debate among technologists and philosophers alike. As AI systems become more capable, they are increasingly trusted with decisions that have significant consequences for individuals and communities. Issues such as algorithmic bias, privacy, and accountability are now central to the discussion. Many experts argue that developing ethical AI is not merely a technical challenge but also a societal one that requires broad public engagement and robust regulatory oversight.

Flow Mastery

Expert · ~35–45 min

Full-length PTE‑style passages with a mix of intensive "that" and authentic content. The goal: read aloud without any self‑correction.

🎯 The "Keep Going" Rule: If you mispronounce a word or lose your place, do not stop. Your fluency score depends on continuous delivery.
⏱️ Suggested: 60–90 seconds per passage

🔹 Intensive "That" Passage (High Frequency)

Read aloud once. No stops. No corrections.
It is widely recognised that technology has transformed the way that students learn, and it is now universally acknowledged that no classroom is untouched by it. However, research suggests that the effects are not uniformly positive, and results have been remarkably similar across very different school systems. The evidence that has been gathered indicates that tools readily available to teachers matter less than how they are used. Approaches that keep human judgement central have become increasingly popular among school leaders. This suggests that the future of education lies in a model where widely recognized best practice and new tools work side by side.

📄 Authentic PTE-Style Full Passages (Natural Frequency)

🎯 Realistic test simulation: These passages have a natural density of "that". Focus on rhythm, intonation, and non-stop delivery.
Passage 1 · The Future of Work
The nature of work is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by technological advancements and shifting societal expectations. Automation and artificial intelligence are increasingly capable of performing tasks that were once considered the exclusive domain of human workers. This has led to widespread concern about job displacement and economic inequality. However, history suggests that technological revolutions ultimately create new categories of employment, even as they render others obsolete. The challenge for modern societies is to ensure that workers have access to the education and training they need to navigate this transition successfully. Policymakers must also consider the social implications of a changing labour market, including the potential for increased precariousness and the need for stronger safety nets.
Passage 2 · Climate Adaptation
While mitigation efforts remain a priority, adaptation to climate change is now recognised as an equally urgent necessity. Communities around the world are experiencing the effects of rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and shifting agricultural zones. Adaptation strategies range from building flood defences to developing drought-resistant crops. The effectiveness of these measures depends on a variety of factors, including local geography, economic resources, and governance structures. International cooperation is crucial, as climate change does not respect national borders. Nevertheless, adaptation must be tailored to specific regional conditions to be truly effective.
Passage 3 · The Psychology of Decision-Making
The study of decision-making has revealed that human beings are not always as rational as classical economic models suggest. Cognitive psychologists have identified a range of biases that systematically influence our choices. Confirmation bias, for example, leads us to favour information that supports our pre-existing beliefs, while loss aversion makes us more sensitive to potential losses than to equivalent gains. Understanding these biases is essential for improving decision-making in high-stakes environments, such as medicine and finance. Interventions designed to counteract these biases have shown considerable promise, particularly when they are embedded in the structure of decision processes themselves.
Passage 4 · The Role of Universities
Universities are facing unprecedented challenges in the twenty-first century. The rise of online education has disrupted traditional models of teaching, while the cost of tuition has become a major political issue. At the same time, the role of universities in society is being re-evaluated. Once seen primarily as institutions for the transmission of knowledge, they are now expected to contribute to economic development and social mobility. This expansion of expectations has placed considerable strain on university budgets and governance structures. Yet, despite these pressures, universities remain vital to innovation and cultural enrichment.
Passage 5 · The Digital Divide
The digital divide refers to the gap between those who have access to modern information technology and those who do not. This disparity is not simply about infrastructure; it also encompasses differences in digital literacy and the ability to use technology effectively. As more services move online, individuals who lack access or skills are at risk of being excluded from essential aspects of modern life, from education to healthcare to employment. Bridging the digital divide requires a multi-faceted approach that includes investment in infrastructure, education, and affordable access.

🎯 Full Flow Passages (New Topics)

🎯 Focus: Read each passage aloud three times without stopping. These cover topics not used in Tabs 1–4.
Passage 1 · Space Exploration & Public Funding
Public funding for space exploration has long been a subject of debate. Advocates argue that the scientific and technological spillovers, from satellite navigation to medical imaging, more than justify the expense. Critics counter that the money could achieve more if redirected toward pressing terrestrial problems such as poverty and disease. The recent surge in private-sector space ventures has complicated this picture further, raising questions about how public agencies and commercial actors should divide responsibility. Whatever the outcome, the pace of investment shows no sign of slowing.
Passage 2 · The Future of Remote Work
Remote work, once a niche arrangement, has become a permanent feature of many industries. Employers report mixed results: some note gains in productivity and employee satisfaction, while others point to a gradual erosion of team cohesion and mentorship opportunities for junior staff. Hybrid models, which combine office and remote days, have emerged as a compromise, though they bring their own coordination challenges. Ultimately, the long-term impact on urban office markets and commuting patterns remains an open question.
Passage 3 · Biodiversity Loss
Biodiversity loss is accelerating at a rate unprecedented in human history, driven largely by habitat destruction, pollution, and climate change. Scientists warn that the disappearance of a single species can trigger cascading effects throughout an ecosystem, given how interdependent food webs tend to be. Conservation strategies increasingly focus on protecting entire habitats rather than individual species, on the grounds that this approach yields more durable results. International agreements exist, but enforcement varies considerably between countries.
Passage 4 · Data Privacy in the Digital Age
As more of daily life moves online, questions of data privacy have taken on new urgency. Companies collect vast quantities of personal information, often with limited transparency about how it will be used. Regulators in several jurisdictions have introduced stricter rules governing consent and data retention, though critics argue that enforcement lags behind the pace of technological change. Individuals, meanwhile, are left to navigate a landscape where convenience and privacy frequently pull in opposite directions.
Passage 5 · Ageing Populations and Healthcare Systems
Healthcare systems across the developed world are grappling with the consequences of an ageing population. Demand for long-term care is rising just as the workforce available to provide it shrinks. Some countries have responded by investing in assistive technology, from remote monitoring to robotics, while others have focused on immigration policy to recruit care workers from abroad. Whichever path is chosen, the financial and logistical pressure on public health budgets is expected to intensify in the coming decades.
