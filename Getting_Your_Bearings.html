The Big Picture

What to expect
  • About 2 hours – one test, one sitting. No scheduled break; if you need one, raise your hand, but the clock keeps running.
  • Fixed order – Speaking & Writing first, then Reading, then Listening. You cannot skip or reorder sections.
  • 22 question types – you'll practice every one of them over the next three days. Today is just the map.

How You're Marked

AI + human review
  • AI scores every task – all 22 task types are scored by Pearson's AI system. The algorithm evaluates your responses against thousands of scored samples.
  • Results delivered in about 48 hours. Scores are valid for two years.
  • Human expert review – six tasks get a second look before your score is final:
    • Describe Image · Retell Lecture · Summarize Group Discussion
    • Respond to a Situation · Write Essay · Summarize Spoken Text
Source: pearsonpte.com/pte-academic/scoring

Live Warm‑Up

Feel the clock · No scoring
⏱️ 40s

Describe the Lounge

Stand in the lounge. Set your phone timer to 40 seconds.

Describe this room to a person who has never entered it. Cover the wall hangings, the seating, the floor, and the window light. Talk about the colours and the mood.

This is close to the real Describe Image task. No scoring, no pressure — just a feel for talking against a clock.

💡 Coach tip: If 40 seconds feels short, that's exactly the point. The real task feels the same way at first. You'll get used to it.
⏱️ 40s · with ambient noise

Describe the Kitchen

Stand in the kitchen. Play your chatter audio. Set your timer to 40 seconds.

Describe this kitchen to a person who has never seen it. Cover the counters, the stove, the sink, and the cupboards. Note the light, the tiles, and the overall feel.

This mimics the real test room with other candidates speaking nearby. No scoring, just volume and focus against the clock.

💡 Coach tip: If 40 seconds feels short, that's exactly the point. The real task feels the same way at first. You'll get used to it.

Top Priority High Priority Medium Priority Warm‑Up

Personal Introduction

Warm‑Up
25s prepare 30s record

This task is not scored, but your recording is sent to the institutions you apply to. Think of it as a first impression.

You will have 25 seconds to prepare and 30 seconds to record a brief personal introduction. Use this to settle your nerves and get comfortable speaking into the microphone before the scored tasks begin.

Home Exercise

Record a 30‑second "hello, this is me" introduction. The goal is simply to get used to talking into a microphone on a timer. Do not overthink the content — focus on speaking clearly and confidently.

💡 Coach tip: Even though it is not scored, institutions may listen to this. Keep it friendly, professional, and within the time limit.

Read Aloud

Top Priority
30–40s prepare read aloud in time given

Read an on‑screen passage aloud in the time given. This is one of the most important tasks in the entire test.

  • Scoring impact: Affects your Reading score, Speaking score, plus your pronunciation and fluency ratings.
  • Use the preparation time to scan the passage for tricky words and plan your pacing.

Home Exercise

Pick a short news paragraph. Read it aloud once for practice, then once for real, timed. On the second attempt, treat it like the real test — no pauses, no restarts. Build the habit of delivering in one clean take.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Affects: Reading + Speaking scores · Pronunciation · Fluency — practise this task daily.

Repeat Sentence

Top Priority
15s to repeat after hearing once

Hear a sentence once, then repeat it back as accurately and fluently as possible.

  • Scoring impact: Affects both Listening and Speaking scores.
  • Key tip: If you lose a word mid‑sentence, keep going. A smooth delivery with one missed word scores better than a broken attempt where you stop and restart.

Home Exercise

Read random sentences from a magazine or app, then repeat them back immediately. No writing allowed — this is purely an ear‑to‑mouth exercise. The goal is to train your short‑term memory and spoken fluency together.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Affects: Listening + Speaking scores · Fluency matters more than perfect accuracy.

Describe Image

High Priority
25s prepare 40s speak

Describe an image in detail — charts, graphs, photographs, maps, or diagrams.

  • Human‑reviewed: Your response is reviewed by a human scorer, so a genuine, natural description beats a memorised script every time.
  • Cover the key elements: title, axes, trends, notable features, and a brief conclusion.

Home Exercise

Describe anything around you — your lounge, a photo on your phone, or even tomorrow's grocery list. Set a 40‑second timer and speak continuously. The goal is to build the habit of speaking fluently for the full duration without running out of things to say.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Content is human‑reviewed — authenticity and structure matter more than template memorisation.

Retell Lecture

High Priority
10s prepare 40s speak

Listen to or watch a short lecture, then retell the main points in your own words.

  • You do not need to capture every detail. Focus on the main idea, key supporting points, and any examples or conclusions the speaker mentions.
  • Use the 10‑second prep time to mentally organise what you heard before you begin speaking.

Home Exercise

Play a 2‑3 minute YouTube clip on any topic — science, history, culture, anything. After it finishes, retell the main points in 40 seconds. Focus on summarising, not transcribing. Use your own words.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Focus on: Main idea · Key supporting points · Examples · Your own words, not the speaker's exact phrases.

Answer Short Question

Medium Priority
10s to answer with one word or a few words

Answer a short question with one word or a few words. No long explanations needed.

  • Important: This task only affects your Listening score, even though you speak your answer. There is no bonus for saying more — keep it brief and accurate.
  • The questions test general knowledge and common vocabulary. Speed and precision matter.

Home Exercise

Do quick‑fire general knowledge questions with a friend or app. Answer instantly — the goal is to build reflexive response speed. Do not overthink. If you do not know, guess and move on.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Affects: Listening score only · Brevity is rewarded — one correct word beats a full sentence.

Summarize Written Text

High Priority
10 minutes one sentence only

Write a one‑sentence summary of a passage. The sentence must be between 5 and 75 words.

Critical: Writing outside the 5–75 word range scores zero on every factor. Count your words before submitting.

The sentence must be grammatically complete — a full stop at the end, proper subject and verb. A fragment or run‑on will hurt your score.

Home Exercise

Pick a short newspaper article. Write a one‑sentence summary in 10 minutes. After writing, check that it is genuinely one sentence and between 5–75 words. Use a word counter — do not guess.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Word limit: 5–75 words · Outside this range = zero on all scoring factors · Always count before submitting.

Write Essay

Medium Priority
20 minutes 200–300 words

Write a 200–300 word essay on a given topic. Content, structure, and language range are all human‑reviewed.

Word limit: Going under 120 or over 380 words scores zero. Stay within 200–300 for a safe buffer.

Plan your time: 2–3 minutes to plan, 15 minutes to write, 2–3 minutes to review. A clear structure with an introduction, body paragraphs, and conclusion works best.

Home Exercise

Pick an opinion topic (e.g. "Should governments ban plastic bags?"). Write for 20 minutes with no outline given beforehand. The goal is to see your natural pacing — how many words you comfortably produce in 20 minutes and where you struggle.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Scored on: Content · Structure · Language range · Vocabulary · Grammar · Spelling

Fill in the Blanks — Dropdown

Medium Priority

Read a text with gaps. For each gap, click the drop‑down list and pick the word that best fits the context.

This task tests your vocabulary and grammatical awareness — can you spot which word makes the sentence correct?

  • Read the whole sentence before choosing. The word must make sense both grammatically and in meaning.

Home Exercise

Take a paragraph and blank out 4–5 words. Write out 2–3 options for each gap — only one should be correct. Give it to a study partner or come back to it later so you do not remember the original text.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Strategy: Read the full sentence first · Check grammar (tense, prepositions, collocations) · Check meaning in context

Lower Priority

Multiple Choice — Single Answer
Lower Priority

Read a text and pick one correct answer. The most straightforward of the reading tasks — read carefully and eliminate obviously wrong options first.

Home Exercise

After reading a short article, ask yourself one comprehension question and answer it. This builds the habit of reading for main ideas and supporting details.

Reorder Paragraph

Medium Priority

You will see several text boxes in random order. Drag them into the correct sequence to form a coherent paragraph.

This task tests your understanding of logical flow, cohesion, and coherence. Look for:

  • Topic sentences — usually come first
  • Linking words — "however," "therefore," "in addition" signal order
  • Pronouns — "this," "it," "they" refer back to earlier ideas

Home Exercise

Take a short paragraph and cut it into 4–5 strips of paper. Mix them up, then put them back in the correct order. Do this with different text types — news articles, opinion pieces, instructions — to get used to spotting logical structure.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Look for: Topic sentences first · Linking words (however, therefore) · Pronoun references · Chronological or cause‑effect order

Fill in the Blanks — Drag and Drop

Medium Priority

Drag words from a pool at the bottom of the screen into the gaps in a text. Some words in the pool are decoys — they will not fit anywhere.

This task tests vocabulary, grammar, and collocation knowledge. You need to know which words naturally go together.

  • Pay attention to word form — the gap may need a noun, verb, adjective, or adverb. The pool may contain different forms of the same root word.

Home Exercise

Same as the Dropdown exercise, but give yourself a pool of 6–7 words for 4–5 gaps. Include 2–3 decoy words that do not fit anywhere. This trains you to evaluate every option carefully rather than just filling gaps.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Watch for: Word form (noun/verb/adjective) · Collocations · Decoy words that look plausible but do not fit

Summarize Spoken Text

High Priority
10 minutes total 50–70 words

Listen to a recording and write a 50–70 word summary. Content is human‑reviewed.

Critical: Writing under 40 or over 100 words scores zero on every factor. Always count your words.

Take notes while listening — jot down key points, then craft your summary from those notes.

Home Exercise

Play a 2‑minute podcast or news clip. Write a 50–70 word summary while listening and just after it finishes. Use a word counter to check your range. Practise condensing spoken content into a tight, accurate summary.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Word limit: 50–70 words · Under 40 or over 100 = zero on all factors · Human‑reviewed for content quality.

Fill in the Blanks — Type In

Medium Priority

Listen to a recording while reading its transcript on screen. Type the missing words into the gaps as you hear them.

This task tests your ability to listen and type simultaneously. You need to hear the word, recognise its spelling, and type it quickly.

  • The recording moves at natural speed — there is no pause. Keep your fingers on the keyboard and your eyes moving ahead in the text.

Home Exercise

Read a paragraph aloud slowly while a friend (or a recording of yourself) types it into a phone. Deliberately mumble or skip 4–5 words. The typist must fill in those gaps from context. Then swap roles.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Strategy: Keep eyes ahead of the audio · Type quickly, correct later · Practise spelling common academic words.

Highlight Correct Summary
Lower Priority

Listen to a recording, then pick the paragraph that best summarizes what you heard. You are choosing between several summary options — only one is accurate.

Home Exercise

Play a clip, then write two summaries — one accurate, one slightly off. Ask a study partner to pick the correct one, or come back later and choose yourself.

Multiple Choice — Single Answer
Lower Priority

Listen to a recording, then pick one correct answer. The most straightforward listening task — listen for the main idea or a specific detail, then select the option that matches.

Home Exercise

After listening to a short clip, ask yourself one quick comprehension question and answer it. Focus on main ideas and key details.

Select Missing Word
Lower Priority

Listen to a recording that ends with a beep. Pick the word or phrase that best completes the sentence from the options given. Context and grammar clues are your guide.

Home Exercise

Read a sentence aloud, then stop before the last word. Guess what word completes it from the context and grammar. Practise with different sentence types.

▶ Watch video guide →

Highlight Incorrect Words

High Priority

Follow along with a transcript on screen while listening. Click on any word in the transcript that differs from what you actually hear.

Scoring warning: Wrong picks cost a point each too. Confidence matters as much as sharp ears — only click when you are sure the transcript is wrong.

The differences are usually subtle — changed numbers, swapped synonyms, or altered verb tenses. Stay focused.

Home Exercise

Read a paragraph aloud but deliberately change 4–5 words. Record yourself. Give the original text to a study partner. They listen to your recording and circle the words that do not match. Then swap roles.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Scoring: Correct picks earn points · Wrong picks lose points · Only click when confident the transcript differs.

Write from Dictation

Top Priority

Listen to a sentence and type it exactly as you hear it — word for word, with correct spelling and punctuation.

This is one of the highest‑scoring tasks in the entire test. Every correct word counts. Even partial credit helps — type what you remember even if you miss a word.

The sentence is played once. Focus, listen carefully, and type quickly while the sentence is still fresh in your memory.

Home Exercise

Read a sentence once at natural speed, then type it from memory word for word. Start with short simple sentences, then gradually increase length and complexity. Check your spelling and punctuation against the original.

▶ Watch video guide →
💡 Top scoring task: Every correct word counts · Type what you remember · Spelling and punctuation matter.

Take It Further

Build resilience

Once you are comfortable with the task types, try practising with background noise.

Play the radio on low, or have a video running in another room. The test centre will not be silent — building resilience early helps.

💡 This is not a scored requirement — just a study habit to build focus and calm under distraction.
